\section*{ACM TOMM Style Review for the Revised Manuscript}

\subsection*{Overall Recommendation}
\textbf{Recommendation: Major Revision, but substantially improved and now closer to the ACM TOMM bar.}

\subsection*{Summary}
This revised manuscript studies background-based location privacy in released video frames and proposes PPEDCRF, a calibrated selective perturbation framework that combines unary sensitivity prediction, dynamic CRF refinement, normalized control penalty, and Gaussian noise injection on inferred location-sensitive background regions. Compared with the previous version, the revision is materially stronger in three respects: (i) it expands attacker-sensitivity analysis beyond ResNet18/50 to VGG16 and CLIP encoders, including an explicitly acknowledged failure mode on CLIP ViT-L/14; (ii) it fills in the temporal-consistency table with concrete flicker and perturbation-stability results; and (iii) it improves the qualitative presentation and the explanation of the DP-style calibration rule.

These revisions address several important weaknesses from the earlier draft. In particular, the paper is now more honest and better balanced: it clearly states that the benchmark is a controlled proxy rather than a large-scale geolocalization benchmark, it does not claim formal output-level differential privacy, and it explicitly acknowledges that the inferred spatial support is the most transferable component of the method. This substantially improves the paper’s credibility.

However, despite this clear progress, the manuscript still falls short of a clean ACM TOMM acceptance in its current form. The remaining weakness is no longer lack of clarity, but \emph{insufficient empirical breadth and incomplete attacker realism}. The paper is now technically coherent and reasonably polished, but its main evidence still comes from a small proxy benchmark built from only 12 paired locations and galleries up to 48 candidates. Moreover, while the added CLIP experiments strengthen the attacker analysis, the evaluation still stops short of established VPR-specific attackers such as NetVLAD, Patch-NetVLAD, CosPlace, or MixVPR, which are directly discussed in the related work and conclusion. Therefore, the current manuscript is much improved and potentially publishable after another strong revision, but it is not yet fully convincing at the ACM TOMM standard.

\subsection*{Major Strengths}
\begin{itemize}
    \item The revised manuscript is significantly more honest and better aligned with the released implementation. The distinction between implementation, motivation, and empirical claim is now much clearer.
    \item The attacker-sensitivity section is substantially improved. Adding VGG16, CLIP ViT-B/32, and especially CLIP ViT-L/14 makes the evaluation much more credible than before.
    \item The paper now includes an explicit failure mode: PPEDCRF does not reduce retrieval under CLIP ViT-L/14 at $\sigma_0 = 8$. This is an important and commendable improvement because it bounds the attacker scope instead of overclaiming robustness.
    \item Temporal metrics are now completed. This resolves one of the most serious problems in the previous draft.
    \item The matched-operating-point analysis is clearer and better structured than before. It now conveys more precisely what PPEDCRF does and does not outperform.
    \item The revised Fig.~5 is much stronger. The added sensitivity heatmap, absolute-difference visualization, and zoomed crop make the release-side mechanism easier to interpret.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Main Remaining Weaknesses}
\begin{itemize}
    \item The empirical basis is still narrow for ACM TOMM. The benchmark remains a controlled proxy with only 12 paired locations and galleries of size at most 48.
    \item The strongest attacker family discussed in the paper’s own literature review---modern VPR models such as NetVLAD, Patch-NetVLAD, CosPlace, and MixVPR---is still absent from the actual experiments.
    \item The main privacy claim is still only partially decoupled from the benchmark construction. Because the paired scenes are mined using ResNet18 similarity, the benchmark may still favor feature families closer to the anchor embedder.
    \item The method contribution is now better positioned, but the experiments still suggest that \emph{support quality} is stronger than \emph{the full PPEDCRF mechanism}. The paper should sharpen this distinction even further.
    \item Some interpretations in the matched-operating-point section remain slightly too favorable toward PPEDCRF given that, at equal PSNR, PPEDCRF and global Gaussian noise often produce the same Top-1 privacy.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Detailed Review and Revision Requests}

\subsubsection*{1. The paper is much stronger, but still not fully at ACM TOMM evidence depth}
The current revision is clearly stronger than the previous one. The added attacker backbones and completed temporal table substantially improve the manuscript. However, the core evidence still comes from a small local paired-scene proxy benchmark. This remains the main bottleneck for publication readiness.

\textbf{Required revision:}
The paper should further tone down any phrasing that sounds broader than the current evidence. The manuscript should consistently frame itself as:
\begin{quote}
a controlled study of release-side privacy--utility trade-offs under a reproducible proxy benchmark, with bounded attacker transfer.
\end{quote}

\textbf{Why this matters for TOMM:}
ACM TOMM reviewers will likely accept a carefully bounded claim, but may still resist a paper whose entire main quantitative story depends on a small synthetic benchmark.

\subsubsection*{2. The added CLIP results are valuable, but VPR-specific attackers are still missing}
This revision adds VGG16, CLIP ViT-B/32, and CLIP ViT-L/14, which is a significant improvement. However, the most relevant retrieval attackers for the stated threat model are still VPR-specific models. Since the paper itself cites NetVLAD, Patch-NetVLAD, CosPlace, and MixVPR, a reviewer can reasonably ask why none of them appear in Table~5.

\textbf{Required revision:}
At minimum, add one strong VPR-oriented attacker to the main or supplementary evaluation, preferably:
\begin{itemize}
    \item NetVLAD or Patch-NetVLAD as a classical retrieval baseline, and
    \item CosPlace or MixVPR as a more modern baseline.
\end{itemize}

If these cannot be added before submission, the paper should explicitly state in the introduction, experiment section, and conclusion that the current attacker study is still limited to classification-style and CLIP-style embeddings, not dedicated VPR models.

\subsubsection*{3. Benchmark construction bias still needs more explicit discussion}
The benchmark pairs are mined using a fixed ResNet18 attacker. This is reproducible and reasonable for a controlled study, but it creates a possible benchmark-construction bias toward that feature family. The added cross-backbone experiments help, but they do not fully remove this concern.

\textbf{Required revision:}
Add one concise paragraph in the experiment section or limitations discussion explicitly stating that:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item the paired-scene benchmark is anchored on ResNet18 similarity,
    \item this may bias the benchmark toward ResNet-like retrieval structures,
    \item the added VGG/CLIP transfer results partly test this issue but do not fully eliminate it.
\end{enumerate}

This point should be made directly, not only implied.

\subsubsection*{4. Contribution positioning is better, but should be sharpened one step further}
The revised manuscript is much better at acknowledging that the DCRF-inferred support is the most transferable asset. This is now stated in the abstract, experiments, and conclusion. That is a major improvement. However, the manuscript still occasionally frames PPEDCRF as if the full mechanism itself is decisively superior.

The actual results suggest a more nuanced hierarchy:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item accurate support localization is the strongest contribution,
    \item selective stochastic perturbation is a controllable operating-point mechanism,
    \item temporal consistency is a secondary practical benefit,
    \item attacker robustness remains bounded.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Required revision:}
Revise the contribution bullets and conclusion wording to make this hierarchy fully explicit. In particular, avoid any sentence that could be read as ``PPEDCRF is the strongest privacy defense overall.'' The results do not support that statement.

\subsubsection*{5. The matched-operating-point section is improved, but the interpretation should be more neutral}
Table~6 is a strong improvement over the previous version, and the structure with three PSNR targets is clear. However, the actual result pattern is important: at matched PSNR, PPEDCRF and global Gaussian noise often achieve the same Top-1 privacy. That means PPEDCRF’s advantage is mainly \emph{budget efficiency at a fixed nominal sigma}, not necessarily stronger privacy at matched utility.

\textbf{Required revision:}
The paper should state this more neutrally:
\begin{quote}
At matched perceptual quality, PPEDCRF and global Gaussian noise produce similar privacy on the current proxy benchmark; PPEDCRF’s main benefit is that it achieves the same utility with perturbation concentrated in sensitive regions rather than across the full frame.
\end{quote}

This would be more faithful to the current evidence than stronger phrasing.

\subsubsection*{6. Temporal-consistency interpretation contains a mild tension}
It is good that Table~7 is now filled. However, the actual numbers show that PPEDCRF, w/o temporal, and w/o NCP are almost identical on flicker and perturbation stability. This makes the temporal contribution appear weaker than the surrounding text suggests.

\textbf{Required revision:}
Please moderate the interpretation in Section~3.6. The results currently support:
\begin{itemize}
    \item PPEDCRF is temporally more stable than random mask and global Gaussian noise;
    \item deterministic baselines are even more stable than stochastic ones;
    \item the current short clips do not strongly separate full PPEDCRF from the temporal ablation.
\end{itemize}

This is still useful, but it is a weaker claim than ``temporal smoothing measurably improves output consistency'' if the ablation numbers are numerically almost unchanged.

\subsubsection*{7. The qualitative figure is now journal-worthy, but could still be made more informative}
Fig.~5 is clearly better than before. The addition of the sensitivity heatmap and the absolute-difference panel is a meaningful improvement. Still, one element could be stronger: the qualitative figure does not yet visually compare PPEDCRF to global Gaussian noise at the same target quality.

\textbf{Suggested revision:}
If space permits, add one extra panel or a supplementary figure showing:
\begin{itemize}
    \item PPEDCRF at a target PSNR,
    \item Global Gaussian noise at matched PSNR,
    \item and optionally blur/mosaic on the same support.
\end{itemize}

This would make the matched-operating-point story visually persuasive rather than purely tabular.

\subsubsection*{8. The DP-style calibration explanation is much improved, but still not fully necessary-looking}
The new paragraph explaining why Eq.~(4) is used over linear scaling or fixed schedules is a good revision. It meaningfully reduces the earlier concern that the DP-style calibration was merely decorative. However, it still stops short of showing whether this choice materially changes the experimental behavior compared with simpler schedules.

\textbf{Suggested revision:}
Add a brief note, either in the main text or appendix, clarifying whether replacing Eq.~(4) with linear scaling would materially alter the frontier, or whether the main effect is interpretability rather than empirical difference. This would make the calibration story more complete.

\subsubsection*{9. The appendix is appropriately conservative, but some of its caveats are important enough for the main text}
The appendix clearly states that the released implementation is intentionally lightweight, that the NCP interpretation is conservative, and that the benchmark is only a proxy. This is good scholarly practice. However, some reviewers may not read the appendix carefully.

\textbf{Required revision:}
Bring one or two of the most important appendix caveats into the main text more explicitly, especially:
\begin{itemize}
    \item that the benchmark is not a replacement for full street-view localization evaluation,
    \item that the current implementation uses a conservative, normalization-based NCP realization rather than a richer symbolic hierarchy.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{10. Publication outlook}
This revised version is \textbf{substantially closer} to ACM TOMM quality than the previous submission. The improvements are real and important. The paper is now coherent, reasonably well evidenced for a proxy study, and much more careful in its claims.

However, I still would not recommend unconditional submission without another revision round, mainly because:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item the attacker family is still incomplete relative to the claimed threat model,
    \item the benchmark remains small and proxy-based,
    \item the contribution hierarchy should be sharpened one step further,
    \item and several interpretations should be made slightly more conservative.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{Actionable Priority List}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Highest priority:} add at least one VPR-specific attacker (NetVLAD / Patch-NetVLAD / CosPlace / MixVPR).
    \item \textbf{Highest priority:} explicitly discuss benchmark-construction bias due to ResNet18-based pairing.
    \item \textbf{High priority:} further sharpen the claim hierarchy so that support localization is clearly the main transferable contribution.
    \item \textbf{High priority:} soften the matched-operating-point interpretation to reflect that PPEDCRF and global noise often tie at equal PSNR.
    \item \textbf{Medium priority:} moderate the temporal-consistency interpretation because full PPEDCRF and its temporal ablation are numerically almost identical.
    \item \textbf{Medium priority:} add a visual matched-PSNR comparison in the qualitative section or supplement.
    \item \textbf{Medium priority:} clarify in the main text which appendix caveats are essential for interpreting the results.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{Bottom Line}
This revision is a substantial step forward. It fixes several serious weaknesses of the previous draft and now presents a credible, bounded, implementation-aligned study of release-side location privacy. The manuscript is no longer blocked by unfinished tables or missing attacker sensitivity. The main remaining issue is \emph{scope}: the evidence is still not broad enough to fully satisfy ACM TOMM without one more strengthening pass. With added VPR-specific attacker evaluation and slightly sharper claim control, this paper could become a realistic ACM TOMM submission.
