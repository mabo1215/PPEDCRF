\section*{Fresh Independent Review: ACM TOMM Evaluation}
\textit{This review was generated independently from the current manuscript source
files (main.tex, appendix.tex, ref.bib) without reference to any prior review
cycle. The evaluation standard is ACM Transactions on Multimedia Computing,
Communications, and Applications (TOMM).}

\subsection*{Manuscript Under Review}
\textbf{Title:} PPEDCRF: Dynamic-CRF-Guided Selective Perturbation for
Background-Based Location Privacy in Video Sequences

\subsection*{Overall Assessment}

The paper proposes PPEDCRF, a release-side sanitization framework that uses a
dynamic CRF to infer location-sensitive background regions, a normalized
control penalty to modulate perturbation strength, and calibrated Gaussian
noise to selectively perturb released video frames against gallery-based
retrieval attackers.

The problem is well-motivated and topically relevant to TOMM.  The positioning
as a calibrated privacy--utility trade-off optimizer is coherent, and the
manuscript is refreshingly transparent about what it does \emph{not} claim
(e.g.\ no formal end-to-end DP guarantee).  However, the current evidence base
falls materially short of what TOMM reviewers would require for publication.
The gaps are concentrated in five areas:

\begin{enumerate}
\item Two analysis sections (4.5 and 4.6) contain \textbf{zero concrete
      numerical results}---they describe analyses in qualitative terms only.
\item The titled method components (DCRF, NCP) produce \textbf{no measurable
      benefit} in the only quantitative ablation table.
\item The benchmark is extremely small (12 paired locations, 48-way gallery)
      and Top-10 retreival saturates at 1.0, leaving almost no discrimination
      power.
\item The reference list cites only \textbf{17 of 99 bib entries}, far too few
      for a TOMM journal paper.
\item The appendix uses \texttt{IEEEtran} instead of \texttt{acmart},
      violating ACM formatting requirements.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{Recommendation}
\textbf{Major Revision before ACM TOMM submission.}

\subsection*{Strengths}
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Clear, honest problem framing.}  The threat model (gallery-based
      retrieval attacker on released frames), the defense objective
      (privacy--utility operating-point selection), and the explicit disclaimer
      against formal DP are stated upfront and maintained consistently.

\item \textbf{Implementation-aligned method description.}  The notation table,
      algorithm box, and equation numbering map directly to the released code,
      eliminating the gap between manuscript claims and implementation that
      plagued earlier drafts.

\item \textbf{Controlled benchmark with hard negatives.}  The paired-scene
      retrieval protocol, while small, uses cosine-similarity-mined pairs and
      distractors rather than random negatives, making the evaluation more
      stress-testing despite its limited scale.

\item \textbf{Honest reporting of baselines.}  The paper does not hide the fact
      that mask-guided blur and mosaic outperform PPEDCRF on raw Top-1
      retrieval.  This candor increases credibility.

\item \textbf{Reproducibility.}  The benchmark is generated by a single
      released script with deterministic pair selection and three noise seeds.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{Major Concerns}

\subsubsection*{M1. Sections 4.5 and 4.6 are text-only placeholders}
Section~4.5 (Matched-Operating-Point Analysis) and Section~4.6 (Temporal
Consistency and Perturbation Stability) contain \textbf{no tables, no figures,
and no concrete numbers}.  They describe comparisons in purely relational
language (``PPEDCRF achieves lower Top-1 retrieval accuracy than global
Gaussian noise'') without ever specifying what those values are.  For a
journal submission, every analytical claim must be backed by quantitative
evidence.

\paragraph{Required revision.}
\begin{itemize}
\item Run the matched-operating-point experiments already coded in
      \texttt{run\_controlled\_retrieval\_benchmark.py} and produce a
      results table for Section~4.5 with columns: method, matched PSNR target,
      achieved PSNR, $\sigma$ or parameter used, Top-1, Top-5.
\item Produce a temporal-consistency table for Section~4.6 with columns:
      method, flicker score, perturbation stability, mask IoU.  The metrics
      are already implemented in \texttt{src/eval/metrics.py}.
\item Add at least one figure: a matched-operating-point frontier plot
      showing all methods at controlled PSNR levels.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{M2. DCRF and NCP produce no measurable empirical gain}
Table~1 reports the following for the default setting ($\sigma_0=8$,
gallery~48):
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lcc}
Variant & Top-1 & PSNR (dB) \\
\hline
PPEDCRF            & $0.722\pm0.048$ & $36.18\pm0.01$ \\
w/o temporal (DCRF)& $0.722\pm0.048$ & $36.17\pm0.01$ \\
w/o NCP            & $0.722\pm0.048$ & $36.17\pm0.01$ \\
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
The differences are in the \emph{third decimal place of SSIM} and nowhere else.
The two title-bearing components contribute zero measurable improvement on the
only quantitative evaluation table.  This is the paper's most critical
credibility gap: a manuscript named after DCRF cannot afford to show that DCRF
does not help.

\paragraph{Required revision.}
\begin{itemize}
\item Test DCRF and NCP at \emph{higher noise scales} ($\sigma_0=16, 24, 32$)
      where the dynamic mask should matter more because per-pixel noise energy
      is larger.
\item Report temporal metrics (flicker score, perturbation stability) per
      ablation variant---these are the metrics where DCRF temporal smoothing
      should shine.  The text in Section~4.6 claims this but provides no
      numbers.
\item If DCRF and NCP still show negligible benefit after these experiments,
      consider whether the title and contribution list overstate their role.
      Rename or downscope if warranted by evidence.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{M3. Benchmark is too small and saturated for journal evidence}
The benchmark has only 12 paired locations and a 48-way gallery.  Top-10
retrieval saturates at 1.000 for \emph{every} method, including the strongest
defenses.  This means the benchmark cannot distinguish between a method that
partially disrupts retrieval and one that is nearly as identifiable as the
raw query.  The effective discriminative range is limited to Top-1 and Top-5,
which for 12 query locations gives very coarse-grained signal.

\paragraph{Required revision.}
\begin{itemize}
\item Scale the benchmark to at least 50--100 paired locations and a 200+
      gallery, using all available monitoring sequences.
\item If the monitoring dataset is too small, supplement with a public
      dataset such as Pitts250k-test, Tokyo~24/7, or Nordland for a
      secondary evaluation track.
\item Report Top-1, Top-5, and Recall@10 on the larger gallery where
      Top-10 is no longer trivially saturated.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{M4. PPEDCRF degrades privacy under ResNet50}
Table~3 shows that under a ResNet50 attacker, PPEDCRF has a \emph{positive}
$\Delta$ ($+0.028$ to $+0.083$), meaning it \emph{increases} retrieval
accuracy compared with raw queries.  The paper correctly flags this as an
attacker-sensitivity issue, but for a TOMM submission the result is still
alarming: the proposed defense can make things worse under a plausible
alternative backbone.

\paragraph{Required revision.}
\begin{itemize}
\item Investigate why ResNet50 benefits from PPEDCRF perturbation.  Possible
      hypotheses:\ denoising effect, feature-space smoothing, or
      benchmark construction bias (pairs mined with ResNet18).
\item Add at least one non-ResNet attacker (e.g.\ VGG16, a ViT-based
      backbone, or a dedicated VPR model like NetVLAD/CosPlace) to test
      whether the negative transfer is ResNet-specific or systematic.
\item If reconstruction of the pair-mining process with a different anchor
      backbone changes the paired set, report those results to disentangle
      benchmark bias from true attacker transfer.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{M5. Reference list is severely under-populated}
The manuscript cites only \textbf{17 of the 99 entries} in \texttt{ref.bib}.
For an ACM TOMM journal paper, 17 references is far below the expected
density.  This is particularly problematic in Related Work, where each of the
seven sub-paragraphs cites only 1--4 papers and skips many closely relevant
works, including:
\begin{itemize}
\item recent video anonymization methods (GANobfuscator, action-preserving
      anonymization, face-swapping pipelines);
\item adversarial perturbation defenses in multimedia;
\item CRF-based segmentation and temporal refinement (DeepLab, etc.);
\item broader DP mechanism design literature beyond the three references cited.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Required revision.}
\begin{itemize}
\item Expand Related Work to approximately 40--60 cited references,
      covering the major ACM/IEEE venues in multimedia privacy, VPR,
      video anonymization, and perturbation-based defense.
\item Remove all unused bib entries from \texttt{ref.bib} to keep the
      file clean.
\item Add comparative discussion paragraphs that position PPEDCRF relative to
      the closest recent works, not just broad category summaries.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{M6. Legacy experiment track is weakly connected}
Section~4.2 retains detector-training curves on MOT16/17, Cityscapes, KITTI,
and VOC2008 from an older pipeline.  These results evaluate foreground
detection/segmentation utility under a different training regime and do not
measure retrieval privacy \emph{at all}.  The paper acknowledges this, but the
section still occupies substantial space and two large figures (Figures~3
and~4) without contributing to the core privacy--utility story.

\paragraph{Required revision.}
\begin{itemize}
\item Either (a)~add at least one fixed downstream-task evaluation on the
      sanitized frames from the \emph{new} benchmark (e.g.\ object detection
      or semantic segmentation inference on PPEDCRF-sanitized frames) to
      demonstrate utility preservation under the same pipeline, or
      (b)~condense the legacy section to a brief paragraph and move the
      figures to the appendix.
\item If option~(a) is chosen, report task accuracy vs.\ PSNR jointly with
      the privacy frontier to give a unified three-axis view (privacy,
      perceptual quality, task utility).
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Minor Concerns}

\subsubsection*{m1. Appendix uses IEEEtran instead of acmart}
ACM TOMM requires all manuscript components to use the \texttt{acmart}
document class.  The appendix currently uses
\verb|\documentclass[trans]{IEEEtran}|.
\paragraph{Fix.} Switch the appendix to \verb|\documentclass[manuscript,screen,review,anonymous]{acmart}| with \verb|\acmJournal{TOMM}| and mirror the main-text preamble.

\subsubsection*{m2. 82 dead bib entries}
\texttt{ref.bib} contains 99 entries; only 17 are cited.  Dead entries cause
no compilation error but are a sign of incomplete cleanup and can confuse
reviewers who inspect the source.
\paragraph{Fix.} Remove all uncited entries or, if they will be cited in the
expanded Related Work, add the corresponding \verb|\cite{}| commands.

\subsubsection*{m3. Figures directory contains $\sim$20 unreferenced images}
\texttt{paper/figs/} has 33 image files, but \texttt{main.tex} references
roughly 8.  Files such as \texttt{050.png}, \texttt{12.png}--\texttt{19.png},
\texttt{GAS.PNG}, \texttt{GAUSSI*.PNG}, \texttt{general*.png},
\texttt{labels*.png}, \texttt{resultsN.png}, \texttt{P\_curve.png},
\texttt{R\_curve.png}, and \texttt{train\_batch*.jpg} appear to be legacy
artifacts.
\paragraph{Fix.} Remove or relocate unreferenced images to reduce repository
clutter.

\subsubsection*{m4. Pipeline figure file name}
Figure~2 is sourced from \texttt{architecture\_of\_solution.png}, which sounds
like a draft placeholder rather than a publication-ready asset name.  The
figure itself is acceptable in content but could benefit from a visual refresh
(sharper text, vector graphics, tighter layout) for a TOMM submission.

\subsubsection*{m5. Abstract length and front-loading}
The abstract is 183 words, which is within TOMM limits but spends its first
two sentences on motivation before reaching the method name.  For a
transactions paper, leading with the problem--solution pair in the first
sentence would improve scanning efficiency.

\subsubsection*{m6. The ``6~dB advantage'' framing is misleading in isolation}
The abstract and conclusion both highlight a ``6~dB PSNR advantage over
indiscriminate global Gaussian noise.''  While technically accurate, this
comparison is against the weakest evaluated baseline.  The more informative
comparison is against mask-guided blur (which achieves stronger privacy at
30.61~dB) and mosaic (stronger privacy at 26.07~dB).  Readers may feel misled
if the strongest result is foregrounded without context.
\paragraph{Fix.} Acknowledge blur/mosaic comparison immediately after stating
the 6~dB advantage in both the abstract and the conclusion.

\subsubsection*{m7. No privacy budget sweep for deterministic baselines}
Blur and mosaic are reported at a single kernel/block size.  Since they use the
same DCRF spatial support, sweeping their intensity parameter (blur radius,
block size) and plotting the resulting frontier alongside PPEDCRF's
$\sigma_0$-sweep would give a fairer multi-method frontier comparison.

\subsubsection*{m8. CCS concepts and keywords}
The CCS descriptors and keywords are reasonable but could be more specific.
Consider adding \texttt{Security and privacy~Location privacy} if it exists in
the ACM CCS tree, and adding ``video anonymization'' or ``visual place
recognition'' as keywords.
