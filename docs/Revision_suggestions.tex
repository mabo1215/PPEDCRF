\section*{ACM TOMM Style Review for \textit{PPEDCRF}}

\subsection*{Overall Recommendation}
\textbf{Recommendation: Major Revision}

\subsection*{Summary}
This manuscript studies background-based location privacy in released video frames and proposes PPEDCRF, a selective perturbation framework combining unary sensitivity prediction, dynamic CRF refinement, normalized control penalty, and Gaussian noise injection on inferred background-sensitive regions. The paper is clearly motivated by a practically relevant threat model: even after removing GPS metadata, a dashcam frame may still reveal location through background visual cues. The revised manuscript is much more implementation-aligned than earlier versions, and it now explicitly avoids overstating a strict end-to-end differential privacy guarantee. The paper also introduces a controlled paired-scene retrieval benchmark and compares PPEDCRF against random masking, global Gaussian noise, and support-aware deterministic baselines such as mask-guided blur and mosaic.

The topic is suitable for ACM TOMM because it concerns multimedia privacy protection, video sanitization, retrieval-based attack evaluation, and privacy--utility trade-off analysis. The paper has several strengths, especially a focused threat model, an implementation-consistent method description, and a more honest interpretation of what the current benchmark can and cannot prove. However, in its current form, the paper still falls short of the level typically expected for acceptance in ACM TOMM. The main issue is not writing quality, but \emph{evidence strength}: the evaluation is still dominated by a small, synthetic, locally mined paired-scene proxy benchmark, while the strongest privacy results are in fact achieved by support-aware deterministic baselines rather than by the proposed stochastic perturbation mechanism itself. As a result, the current paper supports the value of the \emph{support map} more strongly than it supports the superiority of PPEDCRF as a complete method.

\subsection*{Major Strengths}
\begin{itemize}
    \item The problem setting is clear and meaningful: protecting location privacy from background-based retrieval attacks in released driving frames is important and insufficiently studied in prior face/license-plate-centric anonymization work.
    \item The manuscript now states the threat model, released object, and privacy claim boundaries much more clearly. In particular, the paper now explicitly says that the Gaussian calibration is DP-style and does \emph{not} establish formal output-level $(\epsilon,\delta)$-DP for the released video sequence.
    \item The method section is more implementation-consistent than many privacy papers. The pipeline $I_t \rightarrow u_t \rightarrow p_t \rightarrow \alpha_t \rightarrow I_t'$ is readable, and the role of DCRF, NCP, and selective image-space perturbation is now much clearer.
    \item The matched-operating-point analysis is a good addition. It makes the comparison with global Gaussian noise fairer than merely fixing a single noise parameter.
    \item The manuscript is relatively honest about limitations. It acknowledges that attacker transfer is incomplete, that Top-10 retrieval saturates, and that the benchmark is only a paired-scene proxy rather than a full geolocalization benchmark.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Major Weaknesses}
\begin{itemize}
    \item The central empirical evidence is still too narrow for a TOMM paper. The controlled paired-scene retrieval benchmark uses only 12 paired locations and 36 hard distractors in the final default setting, which is too limited to substantiate broad claims about location privacy in multimedia release scenarios.
    \item The strongest privacy results on the main benchmark are achieved by mask-guided blur and especially mask-guided mosaic, not by PPEDCRF itself. This weakens the claimed novelty of the full method and suggests that the true key contribution is the support estimation rather than the stochastic perturbation design.
    \item The attacker analysis is still too weak. Only ResNet18 and ResNet50 are reported in the main sensitivity study, whereas the paper itself cites much stronger VPR-style attackers such as NetVLAD, Patch-NetVLAD, CosPlace, and MixVPR. Without these, the privacy conclusions remain incomplete.
    \item Table~6 still contains \textbf{TBD} entries for flicker and perturbation stability. This is a serious issue. A submission to ACM TOMM should not contain unfinished quantitative tables in the main paper.
    \item The practical meaning of the DP-style calibration is still underdeveloped. Equation~(4) currently functions mainly as a user-facing scaling rule. Without privacy accounting, sensitivity justification, or comparative discussion against alternative calibration rules, it risks appearing mathematically decorative rather than essential.
    \item Some conclusions are still slightly stronger than the evidence warrants. The current experiments support a useful operating-point mechanism on a controlled proxy benchmark, but not yet a convincing general release-side location privacy solution.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Detailed Comments and Revision Requests}

\subsubsection*{1. Contribution Positioning Must Be Sharpened}
The paper should explicitly reposition the contribution hierarchy. Based on the current results, the most defensible contribution is:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item a temporally stable sensitive-background support estimator,
    \item a calibrated selective perturbation framework built on that support,
    \item a controlled proxy benchmark for studying privacy--utility trade-offs.
\end{enumerate}
At present, the manuscript still sometimes reads as if PPEDCRF as a full stochastic mechanism is the main winner, but the experiments show that support-aware blur/mosaic outperform it in raw privacy on the proxy benchmark. This means the paper must distinguish more carefully between:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{support quality},
    \item \textbf{perturbation mechanism quality},
    \item \textbf{calibration convenience}.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Required action:} rewrite the contribution paragraph, abstract, and conclusion so that the manuscript does not overclaim PPEDCRF as the strongest privacy method, but instead frames it as a controllable, high-utility operating-point method whose main transferable asset is accurate support localization.

\subsubsection*{2. The Experimental Evidence Is Not Yet Strong Enough for TOMM}
The current default benchmark is too small and too synthetic. A TOMM paper normally needs broader empirical support. The paired-scene construction is useful as a controlled proxy, but it cannot remain the dominant evidence.

\textbf{Required action:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Add at least one larger-scale external evaluation or semi-external evaluation. Even if a perfect dashcam-to-street-view benchmark is not locally available, you should construct a more realistic retrieval protocol using a public place-recognition or geolocalization dataset.
    \item Evaluate whether the support map generalizes beyond the locally mined paired scenes.
    \item Increase the gallery scale substantially. Current Top-10 saturation shows the ranking task is too easy or too compressed in candidate space.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{What ACM TOMM reviewers will likely ask:} does the method still work when the gallery is much larger, visually more diverse, and built from a realistic cross-view or cross-condition retrieval distribution?

\subsubsection*{3. Stronger Attackers Are Necessary}
The paper already discusses stronger VPR attackers in related work, but the actual evaluation remains limited. ResNet18 and ResNet50 are not sufficient proxies for the current state of place-recognition attack capability.

\textbf{Required action:}
Add attacker-side evaluations with several of the following:
\begin{itemize}
    \item NetVLAD,
    \item Patch-NetVLAD,
    \item CosPlace,
    \item MixVPR.
\end{itemize}

At minimum, one classical VPR descriptor and one modern strong VPR model should be added. If PPEDCRF fails to transfer, that is still publishable, but the manuscript must then reframe itself as a method with attacker-dependent effectiveness rather than implied attacker-agnostic protection.

\subsubsection*{4. Table 6 Cannot Contain TBD Entries}
A main-paper table with unresolved \texttt{TBD} values is not acceptable for submission.

\textbf{Required action:}
Fill in all temporal metrics before submission. If these metrics cannot be computed reliably, remove the table from the main paper and move the temporal-consistency claim to a lighter qualitative discussion. Do not leave placeholder results in a journal submission.

\subsubsection*{5. Temporal Consistency Claims Need Better Support}
The paper claims that DCRF temporal smoothing improves output consistency, but in Table~3 the ablations \emph{w/o temporal consistency} and \emph{w/o NCP} show almost identical Top-1, Top-5, PSNR, and SSIM to PPEDCRF. This makes the temporal component look weak unless Table~6 proves otherwise.

\textbf{Required action:}
\begin{itemize}
    \item Quantify temporal improvement with completed flicker and perturbation stability metrics.
    \item Add a short visual sequence comparison or frame-difference visualization.
    \item Explain why Mask IoU for ``w/o temporal consistency'' is 1.000 while PPEDCRF is 0.998; without explanation this looks counterintuitive and may confuse reviewers.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{6. The DP-Style Calibration Still Needs Better Justification}
The paper does a good job stating that Equation~(4) is \emph{not} a strict DP guarantee. However, after that clarification, the role of the DP formalism becomes weaker.

\textbf{Required action:}
Strengthen the justification of Equation~(4) in one of two ways:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item either treat it explicitly as a heuristic but principled calibration rule and compare it against simpler alternatives, such as linear scaling or variance schedules;
    \item or provide a more formal discussion of sensitivity, clipping bound $C$, and why this calibration is preferable in practice.
\end{enumerate}

Right now, the equation risks being challenged as unnecessary theory if it does not lead to unique empirical or interpretive value.

\subsubsection*{7. Comparison Baselines Should Be More Comprehensive}
The current baselines are reasonable but still incomplete for a journal paper. Since the results suggest that support-aware deterministic obfuscation is very competitive, the paper should broaden that family.

\textbf{Required action:}
Consider adding:
\begin{itemize}
    \item stronger blur parameter sweeps,
    \item adaptive pixelation or region-wise JPEG degradation,
    \item saliency-guided or segmentation-guided background masking,
    \item learned adversarial perturbation baselines if feasible.
\end{itemize}

This is especially important because the current release-side question is essentially about how best to perturb the \emph{right region}. Reviewers will expect broader evidence once the paper itself shows support-aware blur/mosaic are very strong.

\subsubsection*{8. The Utility Story Is Still Slightly Split Across Two Worlds}
The main paper evaluates retrieval privacy on the new controlled benchmark, while the utility evidence for detector/segmenter preservation is largely legacy and moved to the appendix. This improves focus, but it also creates a gap: the privacy benchmark and the downstream utility benchmark are not tightly coupled.

\textbf{Required action:}
Provide at least one downstream task experiment on the \emph{same} or closely related sanitized data used in the new release-side benchmark. Otherwise, the privacy--utility trade-off remains partially assembled from different experimental worlds.

\subsubsection*{9. Qualitative Figure Needs Better Journal-Level Design}
Figure~5 is useful, but currently it still looks more like an internal demonstration figure than a polished journal figure. The red box overlay in particular is somewhat crude for a TOMM submission, and the figure does not explicitly show where privacy-relevant background cues are suppressed relative to location-relevant structures.

\textbf{Required action:}
Redesign the qualitative figure with:
\begin{itemize}
    \item higher-resolution crops,
    \item zoomed callouts on privacy-relevant background regions,
    \item side-by-side difference maps or heatmaps,
    \item clearer legend or color coding.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection*{10. Writing-Level Polishing Is Still Needed}
The manuscript is much improved, but several journal-level polishing issues remain:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Some claims in the conclusion are still stronger than the empirical support.
    \item The benchmark is repeatedly called ``controlled paired-scene'' and ``proxy''; the wording is honest but repetitive. Condense this discussion for readability.
    \item The appendix contains important implementation-alignment caveats. Some of these should be summarized more directly in the main paper so reviewers do not feel key limitations were moved out of sight.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Publication Outlook}
At its current stage, the paper is \textbf{not yet ready for ACM TOMM acceptance}. However, it is \textbf{potentially salvageable for TOMM} if the revision substantially strengthens the empirical section, especially:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item removing all unfinished results,
    \item adding stronger retrieval attackers,
    \item enlarging or externalizing the benchmark,
    \item clarifying that the support map is the strongest contribution,
    \item and tightening the privacy claim to match the actual evidence.
\end{enumerate}

If these issues are not addressed, reviewers may conclude that the paper is better suited to a weaker venue or a workshop-style contribution focused on a controlled benchmark and support-aware sanitization analysis rather than a full journal article.

\subsection*{Actionable Revision Priority List}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Must fix before submission:} fill Table~6, remove all placeholders, reduce overclaiming in abstract/conclusion.
    \item \textbf{High priority:} add stronger VPR attackers and larger gallery evaluation.
    \item \textbf{High priority:} reposition the contribution so that support estimation is central and PPEDCRF is framed as a controllable operating-point method.
    \item \textbf{High priority:} add at least one more realistic public-data retrieval experiment.
    \item \textbf{Medium priority:} unify privacy and utility evaluation on more closely matched data.
    \item \textbf{Medium priority:} improve qualitative figures and temporal-consistency evidence.
    \item \textbf{Medium priority:} justify DP-style calibration more concretely through ablation or theory-light comparison.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{Bottom Line}
The manuscript has improved substantially in clarity, honesty, and implementation consistency. The topic is appropriate for ACM TOMM, and the paper now has a coherent narrative. However, the present empirical evidence still supports a narrower claim than the paper currently wants to make. The strongest result at this stage is that \textbf{accurate, temporally stable support localization is valuable for location-privacy protection}, while the evidence that \textbf{PPEDCRF as a full method} is the best overall solution remains insufficient. A strong revision could make this a credible TOMM submission, but the current version still needs major work.



\section*{Summary of Revision Status}
\textbf{Current status: not yet ready for ACM TOMM submission.}

\textbf{Main reason:} the paper's idea is relevant and the writing has improved, but the evidence base is still too narrow and partially unfinished for a multimedia journal at this level.

\textbf{Three biggest blockers:}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item Table 6 still contains TBD results.
    \item The benchmark is too small/proxy-like to support broad claims.
    \item Stronger privacy is achieved by support-aware blur/mosaic, which means the paper must re-center its novelty claim.
\end{enumerate}
