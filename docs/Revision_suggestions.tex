\section*{ACM TOMM Style Review for the Latest Revised Manuscript}

\subsection*{Overall Recommendation}
\textbf{Recommendation: Minor-to-Major Revision before submission; close to submission quality but not yet fully final.}

\subsection*{Summary}
This manuscript studies background-based location privacy in released video frames and proposes PPEDCRF, a selective perturbation framework combining DCRF-based support inference, NCP-based per-pixel modulation, and DP-style Gaussian calibration. The latest revision is substantially stronger than earlier versions. In particular, the paper now includes eight attacker backbones, distinguishes between the default multi-seed mechanism-analysis track and the unified all-backbone rerun, explicitly discusses benchmark-construction bias, and strengthens the support-localization argument with a support-aware deterministic baseline sweep. These changes materially improve the paper’s credibility and make the empirical narrative much closer to ACM TOMM expectations.

The paper’s strongest current contribution is no longer merely the PPEDCRF mechanism as a whole, but the decomposition it now supports empirically: (i) DCRF-based support localization is the most transferable asset; (ii) selective stochastic perturbation provides a controllable operating-point mechanism on top of that support; and (iii) attacker transfer is bounded, heterogeneous, and benchmark-dependent. This is a much more defensible and journal-appropriate positioning.

That said, the paper still has several issues that should be addressed before submission. The remaining problems are no longer fundamental methodological flaws; instead, they are mostly about internal consistency, precision of interpretation, and final editorial polish. In its current form, the paper is close, but a careful final revision round would still materially improve its reception.

\subsection*{Major Strengths}
\begin{itemize}
    \item The evaluation is now much better aligned with the stated threat model. The addition of dedicated VPR embedders (CosPlace, MixVPR, Patch-NetVLAD) is a major improvement.
    \item The manuscript now explicitly acknowledges benchmark-construction bias due to ResNet18-based pair mining, which improves trustworthiness.
    \item The contribution hierarchy is now much clearer: the paper correctly emphasizes support localization as the most transferable component.
    \item The support-aware deterministic baseline sweep is a strong addition. It isolates the value of support quality from the stochastic perturbation mechanism.
    \item The paper now distinguishes between two evidence tracks: a multi-seed mechanism-analysis track and a unified all-backbone rerun. This is helpful and more transparent than silently mixing them.
    \item The conclusion is now much more disciplined and correctly frames the results as bounded transfer evidence rather than universal privacy robustness.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Main Remaining Weaknesses}
\begin{itemize}
    \item The benchmark is still small and proxy-based: 12 paired locations and galleries up to 48 candidates remain limited for a TOMM paper.
    \item The paper now contains two experiment tracks with different protocols, but this distinction may still confuse reviewers unless stated even more explicitly near the relevant tables and figure captions.
    \item The unified rerun improves consistency across backbones, but using a single-seed robustness table while other key tables are multi-seed leaves some variance-related ambiguity.
    \item The conclusion and abstract are much improved, but a few statements still slightly over-favor PPEDCRF relative to what the matched-operating-point analysis actually shows.
    \item Some terminology remains a bit too favorable, especially around ``temporal consistency'' and ``practical advantage,'' given that full PPEDCRF and its temporal ablation remain nearly indistinguishable under the short-clip protocol.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Detailed Comments and Revision Requests}

\subsubsection*{1. This is now close to ACM TOMM submission quality}
The manuscript has reached a stage where the remaining concerns are mostly about precision and polish rather than structural incompleteness. This is a significant improvement over the previous versions. The current version would likely be taken seriously by reviewers rather than dismissed as under-evidenced.

\textbf{Assessment:} the paper is now a credible ACM TOMM candidate after one more careful revision pass.

\subsubsection*{2. The distinction between the two evidence tracks should be made even more explicit}
The new paragraph in Section~3.1 explaining that Table~3, Table~4, and Table~6 use a default multi-seed setting, whereas Table~5 uses a unified all-backbone rerun, is a very good addition. However, because ACM TOMM reviewers often inspect tables before reading all prose carefully, the paper should repeat this distinction more locally.

\textbf{Required revision:}
Add explicit wording directly in the captions of Table~5 and Fig.~4 that these results come from the unified single-run protocol and are intended for \emph{cross-backbone consistency}, whereas the earlier tables focus on \emph{mechanism behavior under one attacker}. This will reduce the chance of reviewers making inappropriate cross-table numerical comparisons.

\subsubsection*{3. The use of a unified single-seed robustness rerun is acceptable, but should be justified more strongly}
The paper now says that the unified eight-backbone table is reported from a single output directory and should be interpreted directionally. This is honest, but reviewers may still ask why the most important robustness table is not also averaged across seeds.

\textbf{Required revision:}
Add one sentence explaining why the unified rerun is still informative despite being single-seed. For example:
\begin{quote}
Because the unified rerun is used primarily to compare attacker backbones under one fixed protocol snapshot rather than to estimate final operating-point means, we interpret the resulting deltas directionally rather than as definitive seed-averaged point estimates.
\end{quote}

\textbf{Suggested extra improvement:}
If possible, add standard deviations or at least a short appendix note for a subset of the unified rerun backbones to show that the directional conclusions are not purely seed artifacts.

\subsubsection*{4. The matched-operating-point interpretation is now much better, but one sentence should still be softened}
Section~3.5 now correctly states that PPEDCRF and global Gaussian noise often achieve the same Top-1 privacy when matched at the same PSNR. This is strong and appropriately honest. However, the sentence
\begin{quote}
``The DCRF contribution is twofold: it defines where to perturb and provides stable support over time.''
\end{quote}
is still somewhat stronger than the actual evidence for the temporal component.

\textbf{Required revision:}
Change this to wording closer to:
\begin{quote}
The DCRF contribution is clearest in support localization; under the current short-clip protocol, the temporal component mainly helps define a structured support process, but its quantitative effect is only weakly separated from the temporal ablation.
\end{quote}

\subsubsection*{5. The temporal-consistency section is improved and now mostly correct}
This section is now much better calibrated than before. In particular, the latest wording acknowledges that the benchmark is not highly sensitive to the temporal component itself. That is the correct direction.

\textbf{Minor remaining issue:}
The phrase
\begin{quote}
``structured support-aware perturbation is substantially more stable than unstructured alternatives''
\end{quote}
is fine, but the section should make even more explicit that this is mainly a comparison against \emph{random mask} and \emph{global Gaussian noise}, not necessarily evidence that the \emph{full temporal module} is crucial.

\textbf{Suggested revision:}
Add one explicit sentence:
\begin{quote}
Within the structured methods, deterministic baselines remain the temporally smoothest, while the difference between full PPEDCRF and its temporal ablation is negligible on these short clips.
\end{quote}

\subsubsection*{6. The support-aware sweep figure is now very useful}
Figure~5 is a strong addition and the updated caption is much better. It now directly states that the sweep isolates the transferable value of DCRF support localization independent of the stochastic perturbation rule. This is exactly the kind of statement the paper needed.

\textbf{Assessment:} keep this figure as is, or with only minor polishing.

\subsubsection*{7. The conclusion is now largely correct, but one phrase remains slightly too strong}
The conclusion says:
\begin{quote}
``PPEDCRF achieves the same privacy as global noise with far less perceptual damage.''
\end{quote}
This should be treated carefully. According to the matched-operating-point section, when utility is held constant, PPEDCRF and global noise often \emph{converge} in Top-1 privacy. The true advantage is better quality at the same nominal sigma, not necessarily better privacy at matched utility.

\textbf{Required revision:}
Rephrase this idea more precisely, for example:
\begin{quote}
At matched utility, PPEDCRF and global Gaussian noise often converge in Top-1 privacy; PPEDCRF’s practical advantage lies in spatially concentrated perturbation, which preserves substantially higher perceptual quality at the same nominal noise scale.
\end{quote}

\subsubsection*{8. The abstract is strong, but could be one step tighter}
The abstract is much improved and now properly includes heterogeneous transfer, neutral and adverse cases, and bounded interpretation. This is a major strength.

\textbf{Minor issue:}
It is slightly dense and tries to report too many backbone-specific facts. For a journal abstract, this may feel overloaded.

\textbf{Suggested revision:}
Reduce one or two architecture-specific clauses and replace them with a more compact summary such as:
\begin{quote}
Transfer to dedicated VPR embedders is broadly supportive but heterogeneous; adverse transfer remains under stronger vision-language encoders and larger galleries.
\end{quote}

This would improve readability without losing rigor.

\subsubsection*{9. The paper should more explicitly frame what remains missing for future work}
The current future-work paragraph is reasonable, but it could be sharpened to better anticipate reviewer objections.

\textbf{Required revision:}
Explicitly mention three remaining limitations:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item the small number of paired locations,
    \item the lack of large cross-view or street-view benchmark evaluation,
    \item and the absence of seed-averaged all-backbone reruns.
\end{enumerate}

This would make the paper appear more complete and self-aware.

\subsubsection*{10. Appendix status}
The appendix remains appropriate and conservative. It does not need major changes. It now plays the right supporting role.

\subsection*{Actionable Priority List}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Highest priority:} strengthen the local explanation around Table~5 and Fig.~4 that these are from a unified single-run protocol and should be interpreted directionally.
    \item \textbf{High priority:} slightly soften the remaining matched-operating-point language so that PPEDCRF is not implied to be better at matched utility.
    \item \textbf{High priority:} refine the temporal-consistency wording so that the structured-vs-unstructured advantage is clearly separated from the full-vs-ablated temporal-module comparison.
    \item \textbf{Medium priority:} tighten the abstract by reducing architecture-specific overload.
    \item \textbf{Medium priority:} add one more sentence in the conclusion explicitly listing what remains unvalidated in future work.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection*{Bottom Line}
This manuscript is now close to submission quality for ACM TOMM. The remaining issues are mostly editorial and interpretive rather than structural. The paper now presents a coherent, bounded, implementation-aligned study of release-side location privacy, with stronger attacker coverage and a much more defensible contribution hierarchy than before. With one more careful polishing round, it would be reasonable to submit.
